  • Rahul Singh Gautam
    One of journalism's basic rules: Be sensitive while reporting on grief and tragedy. Yet large sections of the Western media have abandoned that rule while covering India's Covid disaster. They draw encouragement from India's own unethical media which swarms victims like vultures.
    The media has an obligation to show the true extent of a tragedy. But this must be done without being insensitive to the human suffering. This rule is followed when a tragedy occurs in the West. So why callously show graphic imagery dehumanizing the victims in overseas reporting?
    Cremation is a very private affair in India. Showing graphic images of bodies burning on pyres represented a horrible invasion of privacy. Intrusive, voyeuristic and ratings-driven media coverage amounts to exploiting human suffering. Good journalism must rise above shock value.
    Just 2 months ago, the US was reporting over 5,000 Covid deaths daily (still a world record), while the virus in India seemed to be in retreat. Now Indias devastating second wave (with the UK and Brazil variants in the lead) shows why a global response to the pandemic is vital.
    The pandemic highlights our interconnected world, yet no effort has been made to mount a concerted global response to contain it. Nationally focused responses, especially in the wealthy nations, could allow more contagious transnational variants to compromise vaccinated immunity.
    Rahul
